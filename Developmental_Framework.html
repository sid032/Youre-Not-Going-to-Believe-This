The Complete Editing Framework: Developmental, Structural & Visionary Lenses

A framework for reviewing Drew’s manuscript “You’re Not Going to Believe This” — and any unconventional first-person work that blends memoir, paranormal, travelogue, and philosophical inquiry.

Preamble: The Editor’s Posture Before Reading

Before asking a single question of the text, establish your stance. A world-class editor enters a manuscript not as a corrector but as a first reader with surgical vocabulary — someone who can name what’s happening in the reader’s nervous system at any given moment.

Your job is not to make the book different. Your job is to help the book become more fully itself. This distinction matters enormously for a manuscript like Drew’s, which has a raw, unpolished, conversational voice that is simultaneously its greatest vulnerability and its greatest asset.

The questions below are organized by lens. Not all apply to every manuscript. But for this book — a genre-defying hybrid of autobiography, paranormal testimony, travel writing, and philosophical rumination — these are the lenses that matter most.

LENS ONE: Developmental — Does the Book Know What It Is?

The foundational lens. Before you can edit structure, you need to know what the book is trying to be. A memoir has different demands than a paranormal investigation than a collection of essays. This book seems to want to be all three — and that’s either a revolutionary strength or a fatal confusion.

Core Identity Questions

  1. What is the book’s DNA? Strip it to one sentence. Is it:

    • “A memoir of a life so strange that the author can only make sense of it through the paranormal”
    • “A collection of true paranormal encounters framed by an autobiographical journey of trauma and recovery”
    • “A philosophical investigation into coincidence, consciousness, and the unexplained — told through one man’s improbable life”
    • Something else entirely?

  2. Does the book know what it is? The title “You’re Not Going to Believe This” suggests a framing device — the author is perpetually anticipating the reader’s skepticism. But is skepticism the subject, or is the author just using it as permission to tell whatever story comes next? The difference matters for every editorial decision that follows.

  3. Who is this book for? Be specific. Not “people who like paranormal” but:

    • The skeptical-but-curious reader who disbelieves everything but wants to be transported anyway?
    • The believer seeking validation?
    • The literary reader who loves voice-driven nonfiction?
    • Someone who has experienced the unexplainable and feels alone in it?
    • The answer determines what needs to be in the book and what can be cut.

  4. What is the reader’s payoff? After reading this book, the reader should feel/think/believe what that they didn’t before? If the answer is “I don’t know, it’s just a collection of cool stories” — that’s a developmental problem, not a structural one.

Promise & Delivery Questions

  1. What does the Introduction promise? The current introduction offers “a mixture of literary genres,” a life story, and a collection of “believe it or nots.” Does the rest of the book deliver exactly that? Or does it drift? Flag specific places where the book becomes something different from what was promised.

  2. Where does the book betray its own thesis? The author says “all these stories are true.” But some stories are told with embellishment acknowledged, others with absolute sincerity, others as what “could be considered fiction, magical realism but not quite.” Is this a coherent stance or a contradiction? A world-class editor doesn’t resolve the contradiction — they ensure it’s intentional rather than accidental.

  3. What is missing? The author mentions a coma at age 5 as formative. Part I covers the childhood, the abuse, the music career. But is there a gap between what happened and what the author makes of what happened? A developmental edit identifies where the book needs more reflection, not just more incidents.

LENS TWO: Structural — Does the Architecture Support the Experience?

Once you know what the book wants to be, you can evaluate whether its architecture serves that ambition. Structure is not about chapters being in the right order — it’s about the reader’s emotional and intellectual journey having intentional shape.

Macro-Structure: The Book’s Skeleton

  1. Open a blank page and map the book’s emotional arc. Not the chapter list — the reader’s feeling at each stage. Where does it:

    • Hook the reader?
    • Build tension?
    • Release or resolve?
    • Plateau?
    • Peak?
    • Land? Now compare this emotional map to the chapter list. Where do they conflict? Where does the reader feel something the author didn’t intend?

  2. The Part I / Part II divide — is it working? Part I (autobiography) and Part II (the stories). This is the book’s most consequential structural decision. Does Part I provide necessary context for Part II, or does it exhaust the reader before the “real” content begins? Would the book be stronger if the stories were interwoven chronologically rather than segregated? What would be lost or gained?

  3. Is there a stronger organizing principle? Currently organized as autobiography → stories. Other possibilities:

    • Thematic — group stories by type of phenomenon (ghosts, UFOs, coincidences, premonitions)
    • Chronological — weave autobiographical context + stories together by age/life stage
    • Tension-escalation — start with the least unbelievable story, build to the most
    • Geographic — organize by place (travelogue logic) Each has different implications for reader engagement. Which serves the book’s identity best?

  4. Where does the book lose the reader? Be merciless. Identify the exact paragraphs or chapters where:

    • The energy drops
    • The narrative stalls
    • The author repeats territory already covered
    • The reader asks “why am I being told this?” These are structural problems even if each individual sentence is fine.

Micro-Structure: Chapters & Sequences

  1. How does each chapter earn its place? For every chapter, answer: “If this chapter were removed, what would the reader lose?” If the answer is “an entertaining story but nothing essential to the book’s purpose” — the chapter needs either integration or excision.

  2. Are the chapters the right length? A book that alternates between 3-page chapters and 30-page chapters creates a rhythm. Is that rhythm intentional? Does it serve the content? Or do some chapters drag while others feel rushed?

  3. Where does the book start too early or end too late? Many memoirs begin with childhood when the real story starts later. Many books end after the climax when they should have stopped a chapter earlier. Where are this book’s unnecessary thresholds?

  4. How does the book handle transitions? What happens between Part I and Part II? Is there a bridge, or does the reader suddenly find themselves in a different kind of book? Are the shifts between autobiography and paranormal story within chapters signaled or jarring?

Pacing & Rhythm

  1. Where does the book need compression? Identify the sections that are overwritten — where the same point is made three different ways, where a story has more detail than its significance warrants, where the author has fallen in love with a digression.

  2. Where does the book need expansion? Identify the sections that are underwritten — where a pivotal moment flies by in a sentence that deserves a page, where the author skips over emotional terrain that the reader needs to experience.

  3. What is the ratio of scene to summary? A healthy memoir has both: vivid scenes (showing) and reflective summary (telling). Where is the balance off? Too much summary makes the book feel like a lecture. Too much scene without reflection makes it feel like a scrapbook.

LENS THREE: Visionary — What Is This Book Trying to Become?

The visionary lens asks the most dangerous question: is the book reaching as far as it could? This is not about making the book “better” in a conventional sense. It’s about helping the book fulfill its own highest potential — which may look nothing like a bestseller or a literary prize.

The Book’s Inner Logic

  1. What is the book’s theology / cosmology / philosophy? The author says the experiences have activated “a sense of global consciousness, angelic deliverance, trust in the universe.” But is this belief system named and explored, or is it just gestured at? A visionary edit pushes the author to articulate what they actually believe — not what they think they should believe, or what sounds right, but the messy, contradictory, lived truth of what these experiences have taught them.

  2. Where is the author holding back? The book’s title suggests a constant anticipation of disbelief. Is the author protecting themselves from the reader’s judgment by pre-emptively dismissing their own material? Where does this protective stance prevent the book from reaching emotional depth? A world-class editor can tell the difference between honest skepticism about one’s own experiences and defensive self-deprecation that keeps the reader at arm’s length.

  3. What is the book not saying? Sometimes the most important content is what’s absent. The author mentions a coma at age 5, an abusive mother, a complex relationship with an alcoholic parent, a music career, encounters with the unexplained. But where is the emotional processing? Where does the author feel about what happened, rather than just reporting what happened? The visionary edit asks: what does the author need to say that they haven’t yet admitted to themselves?

  4. Is the book brave enough? A book that presents “unbelievable” stories to a skeptical audience requires courage. But is the author’s courage evenly distributed? Where does the book get timid? Where does it pull punches? Where does it back away from a claim it seemed to be building toward? And conversely — where is it brash when it should be humble?

The Reader’s Transformation

  1. What happens to the reader’s worldview? A great book changes the reader. Not necessarily their beliefs, but their relationship to what they read. Does “You’re Not Going to Believe This” want the reader to:

    • Believe in the paranormal?
    • Suspend disbelief for the duration of the book?
    • Question what they thought they knew about reality?
    • Empathize with someone who has had experiences they can’t explain?
    • Accept mystery as a legitimate response to the unexplained? The answer determines what the book is doing to the reader at every turn.
  2. Where does the book trust the reader vs. over-explain? The author sometimes tells the reader what to think: “This is amazing,” “You won’t believe this,” “I know this sounds crazy.” A visionary edit asks: are these signals helping the reader navigate, or are they preventing the reader from having their own reaction?

LENS FOUR: Artistic & Craft — Does the Writing Sing?

The artistic lens is not about fixing grammar. It’s about whether the author has found the right language for the material. For a raw, conversational voice like Drew’s, the question is not “is this proper English?” but “does this language earn its place on the page?”

Voice & Authenticity

  1. Where does the voice ring most true? Find the sentences that feel most like Drew — the ones that couldn’t have been written by anyone else. What do they have in common? What’s the DNA of the authentic voice? Use those as the benchmark for the rest of the manuscript.

  2. Where does the voice falter? Where does the author slip into cliché, borrowed language, “writerly” prose that doesn’t match the conversational register? Where does the author try to sound like a Real Writer™ instead of like themselves? These moments break the spell.

  3. What is the book’s relationship to “literary” language? The author has a distinctive voice — informal, direct, sometimes profane, sometimes poetic. But there are moments of more formal register (“I became interested in sociology through a literary chance encounter with a book”). Do these register shifts serve the material, or do they create a sense that the author is wearing someone else’s clothes?

Imagery & Language

  1. Where is the language most vivid? The best writing in this manuscript creates images that stick in the reader’s mind. Where are those moments? Can they be used as a model for scenes that are currently under-described?

  2. Where is the language abstract or generic? When the author tells rather than shows, the writing loses power. Flag specific paragraphs where the reader can’t see what’s happening — where the author says “it was amazing” instead of describing what made it amazing.

  3. How does the book handle the ineffable? The author is describing experiences that by their nature resist description (near-death, paranormal encounters, altered states). Where does the book find language that approximates the ineffable, and where does it fall back on received language? The difference between “I felt a presence” (generic) and “the air in the room changed density, like walking through a cobweb you can’t see” (specific) is the difference between telling and evoking.

Opening & Closing

  1. Does the first page earn the next 300? The first page is a contract with the reader. Does this book’s opening deliver intrigue, voice, and a reason to keep reading? Or does it warm up for too long before finding its stride?

  2. Does the last page earn everything that came before? The ending doesn’t need to resolve everything — but it needs to feel intentional. A reader who finishes the book should feel that the journey had a destination, even if the destination is mystery itself. Where is the book’s actual ending? Is there a stronger place to stop?

LENS FIVE: Commercial & Positioning — Where Does This Book Live?

This lens is often neglected by editors who see it as “marketing.” But a book that doesn’t know where it belongs in the world cannot find its readers. And a book that doesn’t find its readers cannot do its work.

  1. What shelf does this book sit on? In a bookstore, would it be in Memoir? Body/Mind/Spirit? New Age? Paranormal? Travel? True Crime adjacent? The answer has implications for cover design, blurbs, categorization, and — crucially — reader expectations that the book must either meet or consciously subvert.

  2. What books sit beside it? What are the comp titles — not just “books like this” but books that this reader would have on their shelf? Authors to consider: Mike Clelland (The Messengers), Whitley Strieber (Communion), John E. Mack (Abduction), Michael Pollan (How to Change Your Mind), Rob Delaney (Mother, Wife, Complicated — for raw voice), Augusten Burroughs (Running with Scissors — for voice + dark childhood). The comps reveal the book’s territory.

  3. What is the author’s platform? For a memoir/paranormal hybrid, the author’s credibility is partly built within the book (by the voice, the honesty, the details) and partly external (who is this person, why should I trust them?). What does the book need to do to establish the author’s authority — not as a scientist or investigator, but as a witness?

  4. Is the title working? “You’re Not Going to Believe This” is provocative and establishes the adversarial relationship with the reader’s skepticism. But it also primes the reader to disbelieve. Is that the right energy for the book to carry? Does the title make a promise the book can keep, or set up a barrier the book has to fight through on every page?

LENS SIX: The Reader’s Interior — Moment-to-Moment Experience

This lens tracks what happens in the reader’s body and mind as they move through the book. It’s the most practical lens for page-level editing because it identifies exactly where the reader’s attention drifts, snags, or intensifies.

  1. Where is the reader confused? Not “where is the writing unclear” but “where does the reader not know where they are in time, space, or narrative?” This is especially critical for a book that jumps between decades and between the mundane and the paranormal.

  2. Where is the reader skeptical in a way that serves the book, and where does skepticism break the spell? The title invites skepticism. That can be a feature — the reader questioning alongside the author. But there are moments where skepticism becomes distraction — where the reader stops engaging because the story hasn’t earned their buy-in. Identify the boundary.

  3. Where does the reader get bored? It’s the editor’s most taboo question and the most necessary one. Boredom is not the reader’s failure. It’s the manuscript’s signal that something needs to change — pacing, content, or approach.

  4. Where does the reader put the book down? Be honest. Are there natural stopping points that feel satisfying? Are there unnatural stopping points (mid-story, mid-thought) where the reader is forced to pause?

  5. Where does the reader smile, laugh, or feel genuine emotion? Identify these moments. They are the book’s oxygen. Don’t edit them. Protect them.

  6. Where does the reader feel manipulated? If the reader senses that the author is pushing an agenda, softening a story, or writing for effect rather than truth, trust collapses. Flag moments that feel performed rather than felt.

LENS SEVEN: Genre Consciousness — What Rules Is This Book Breaking?

Every genre has implicit rules. Breaking them can be visionary. Breaking them accidentally is a mess. This lens helps the author break rules intentionally.

  1. Is the book aware of the genre conventions it’s subverting? A paranormal memoir usually:

    • Builds toward a central revelation
    • Takes the paranormal seriously without irony
    • Establishes the author as a credible witness
    • Has a clear through-line of spiritual transformation This book does some of these and not others. Which deviations are intentional and which are accidental?

  2. Where does the book borrow from genres it doesn’t fully commit to? There are elements of travelogue, comedy, literary fiction, philosophy. Each genre brings reader expectations. Where does the book raise an expectation (this is going to be a travel story about Peru) and then abandon it?

  3. Is the humor working? The author has a self-deprecating, sometimes profane sense of humor. Humor in paranomal writing is risky — it can charm or it can undermine. Where does the humor enhance the reader’s trust, and where does it signal that the author doesn’t take their own material seriously enough for the reader to invest?

LENS EIGHT: The Author’s Arc — Does the Book Serve the Writer?

This lens is unique to memoir and personal nonfiction. The act of writing is itself transformative. A world-class editorial process should help the author become the person who can write this book — not just fix what’s on the page.

  1. What is the author trying to accomplish by writing this book? Beyond “tell my story” — what does Drew need from this process? Validation? Meaning-making? Connection with others who’ve had similar experiences? Is the book achieving that for him? If not, no amount of editing will fix it.

  2. Is the author ready for the book they’re writing? A memoir about trauma and unexplainable experiences requires the author to have done their own work — therapy, integration, distance. Where does the manuscript suggest material that’s too raw or too unprocessed for public consumption?

  3. What would the author regret not including? This is the final, most humane question an editor can ask. If the book goes to press and the author thinks “I wish I’d included…” — the editor has failed. Not because every story must be told, but because the author’s relationship to the finished book will last longer than any editorial decision.

The Editor’s Final Synthesis

After applying all eight lenses, a world-class editor writes a single document that:

  1. Names the book’s deepest ambition — in the author’s language, not the editor’s jargon
  2. Identifies 3-5 structural/developmental priorities — not a list of every issue, but the few changes that would unlock the most progress
  3. Raises the hardest questions — the ones the author may be avoiding
  4. Celebrates what’s working — specifically, concretely, with evidence from the text
  5. Recommends a path forward — what to read, what to try, what to risk

The editor’s job is not to make the book safe. It’s to help the book become dangerous in the right ways.
